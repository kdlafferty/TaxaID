# TaxaAssign: Prior-Likelihood Matching Strategy

## Overview

TaxaAssign computes posterior probabilities for taxonomic assignments given a likelihood
(from TaxaMatch) and a prior (from TaxaExpect). For observed species — those present in
both the likelihood and prior objects — matching is direct. This document covers the
more complex cases that arise when unmatched likelihoods and dark diversity priors must
be reconciled.

---

## Terminology

**Matched species:** A taxon with both a likelihood (from TaxaMatch) and a named prior
(from TaxaExpect). Standard Bayesian updating applies directly.

**Unmatched likelihood:** A taxon for which TaxaMatch has computed a likelihood but
TaxaExpect has no named prior. This occurs when a species has no GBIF records in the
study area. It requires a prior drawn from the dark diversity pool.

**Dark diversity prior:** An anonymous prior generated by TaxaExpect to represent
undetected species. There are two types: singleton mirrors (one per singleton in the
training data, habitat- and location-specific) and the global floor (one per analysis,
always present, effort-based). See `MODELING_STRATEGY.md` in TaxaExpect for how these
are generated.

---

## The Four Matching Cases

### Case 1: One Unmatched Likelihood, One Dark Diversity Prior

Direct 1:1 assignment. The single available dark diversity prior is assigned to the
unmatched likelihood. Standard posterior computation proceeds.

### Case 2: One Unmatched Likelihood, Multiple Dark Diversity Priors

The unmatched likelihood is assigned a prior equal to the **habitat-and-location-filtered
average** of available dark diversity priors. Filtering proceeds as follows:

1. Retain only dark diversity priors whose habitat matches the prediction site habitat.
2. Among those, weight by spatial proximity to the prediction site (closer singleton
   sources contribute more).
3. Compute the weighted mean theta as the effective prior mean.
4. Compute the weighted mean SD as the effective prior SD.

The global floor prior is always included in the average regardless of habitat or
location, ensuring a non-zero contribution even when no habitat-matched singletons exist.

### Case 3: Multiple Unmatched Likelihoods, One Dark Diversity Prior

All unmatched likelihoods share the single dark diversity prior. Each unmatched species
receives the same prior theta. The likelihoods still differentiate among them in the
posterior — the species with the highest likelihood will have the highest posterior — but
absolute posterior probabilities will all be low, reflecting genuine uncertainty about
which (if any) undetected species is responsible for the observation. This is the correct
behavior.

### Case 4: Multiple Unmatched Likelihoods, Multiple Dark Diversity Priors

This is the most complex case. The dark diversity pool represents an exchangeable set of
unknowns — TaxaExpect cannot identify which dark diversity proxy corresponds to which
unmatched likelihood, because dark diversity species have no taxonomic identity. Imposing
a ranked matching (e.g., highest likelihood to highest prior) would introduce a false
correlation between two independently derived orderings.

The recommended approach is:

1. Filter the dark diversity pool to the relevant habitat and location as in Case 2.
2. Compute a single effective prior (weighted average) from the filtered pool.
3. Assign this same effective prior to all unmatched likelihoods.
4. Compute posteriors independently for each unmatched likelihood against the shared
   effective prior.

This treats the unmatched likelihoods as competing hypotheses over the same uncharacterized
probability mass. The species with the highest likelihood receives the highest posterior,
but no artificial correlation is imposed between the prior ordering and the likelihood
ordering.

**Note on the sum of dark diversity priors:** The prior assigned to an unmatched species
is the *average* (or weighted average) of dark diversity priors, not the sum. Summing
would overstate the prior probability of detection for an undetected species and would
not be a valid prior for any single species.

---

## Exchangeability Assumption

The matching logic above rests on treating dark diversity priors as **exchangeable** —
any dark diversity proxy could represent any unmatched likelihood. This is appropriate
because:

- Dark diversity species have no taxonomic identity; they are defined only by their
  habitat, location, and inferred theta.
- The likelihoods from TaxaMatch are derived from sequence similarity, independent of
  the ecological properties captured by TaxaExpect.
- Imposing a ranked correspondence between ecological priors and sequence likelihoods
  would require a cross-model assumption (more similar sequences belong to more locally
  common species) that is not generally justified.

If future evidence supports a systematic relationship between sequence divergence and
local rarity (e.g., highly divergent sequences consistently correspond to rare species),
this assumption could be revisited in a later version.

---

## The Global Floor Prior

TaxaExpect always generates one global floor prior with theta = 1 / N_total, where
N_total is total community sampling effort across all grid × habitat cells. This prior:

- Is always present regardless of whether singletons exist
- Ensures TaxaAssign always has at least one dark diversity competitor in the pool
- Has a deliberately wide SD reflecting near-total uncertainty
- Participates in all habitat-filtered averages (Cases 2 and 4) as a baseline contributor

In datasets with zero singletons (e.g., heavily filtered or very small datasets), the
global floor is the only dark diversity prior and Cases 1 and 3 apply exclusively.

---

## Implementation Notes

- Dark diversity priors in the TaxaExpect output object are flagged with
  `dark_diversity = TRUE` and `dark_diversity_type` ("singleton_mirror" or "global_floor").
- Habitat and spatial filtering in Cases 2 and 4 should use the same habitat labels and
  grid coordinate system as the main prior object.
- Spatial weighting for proximity can use inverse distance or a Gaussian kernel; the
  kernel bandwidth should default to the grid cell size used in TaxaExpect and be
  user-adjustable.
- The effective prior mean and SD passed to TaxaAssign should be stored in the output
  so the matching decision is auditable.

## Prior Parameterization and Posterior Computation

### Alpha/Beta as the Canonical Format

All priors received from TaxaExpect are parameterized as Beta(alpha, beta), not as
mean and SD. This is the canonical format throughout the TaxaID ecosystem. Mean and SD
can be derived on demand for display purposes:

    mean = alpha / (alpha + beta)
    SD   = sqrt(alpha * beta / ((alpha + beta)^2 * (alpha + beta + 1)))

The `compute_posterior` function should accept alpha/beta directly and not expect
mean/SD as inputs.

### Conjugate Posterior Update

The beta-binomial conjugacy makes posterior computation analytical — no numerical
integration is required. If the prior is Beta(alpha, beta) and the likelihood
contributes k successes (matching reads) in n trials (total reads), the posterior is:

    Beta(alpha + k, beta + n - k)

This applies to all matched species (Case 1 and the standard matched case).

### Dark Diversity Prior Values

- **Global floor:** arrives as Beta(1, N_total - 1), where N_total is total community
  count across all grid × habitat cells in the TaxaExpect training data. This is an
  effort-scaled Jeffreys-adjacent prior representing a species expected to appear once
  across the entire sampling effort.
- **Singleton mirrors:** arrive as Beta(alpha, beta) derived from the singleton's
  observed theta via method of moments.

### Averaging Dark Diversity Priors (Cases 2 and 4)

When multiple dark diversity priors must be averaged to produce a single effective prior
(Cases 2 and 4), averaging should be performed in alpha/beta space using moment-matching,
not in mean/SD space. Compute the weighted mean of alpha values and weighted mean of
beta values separately, using spatial proximity weights as described in the Cases 2
and 4 sections above.

## Open Questions (v1)

- Should the spatial weighting kernel bandwidth be fixed at grid cell size, or estimated
  from the data (e.g., median nearest-neighbor distance among singletons)?
- In Case 4, should the weighted average account for the number of dark diversity priors
  (more priors = more uncertainty = wider effective SD)?
- If the number of unmatched likelihoods greatly exceeds the number of dark diversity
  priors, should a warning be issued to the user?
